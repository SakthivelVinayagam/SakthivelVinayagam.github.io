Weather–Crime Trends Analysis (Colchester, UK)

Analysed 16k+ Colchester crime records alongside local weather data to reveal seasonal and spatial trends. Built interactive dashboards and animated visualisations in R (ggplot2, plotly, leaflet) to uncover hotspots and highlight weak-to-moderate associations between weather factors and crime categories.

  • Analysis (HTML)
  • GitHub

R · Data Visualisation · Time Series · Spatial Analysis · Interactive Dashboards

Overview

This project explores how crime patterns in Colchester vary with time, seasonality, and weather. The pipeline ingests monthly crime reports and daily weather observations, aligns them by date and area, and produces interactive visuals for exploratory analysis and stakeholder reporting.

  • Goal: Identify seasonal/temporal patterns and potential weather associations.
  • Scope: ~16k crime records across categories; daily weather aggregated to monthly features.
  • Stack: R (tidyverse, ggplot2, plotly, leaflet).
  • Outputs: Time-series charts, category breakouts, animated seasonality, and hotspot maps.

Data & Processing

  • Crime data: UK Police open data (location, month, crime type).
  • Weather data: Local daily temperature, precipitation, wind; aggregated to month.
  • Preprocessing: de-duplication, geo-filtering to Colchester area, date alignment, mapping to LSOA/ward where available.
  • Features: monthly totals per crime type; seasonal flags; weather summaries (mean temp, total rain); school-holiday indicator (optional).

Methodology

I used an EDA-first workflow in R to visualise crime over time and space, then examined correlations with weather features. The focus is on explainable, stakeholder-friendly visuals rather than causal claims.

  • Temporal: monthly time series, seasonal decomposition (STL), rolling means.
  • Spatial: choropleths and hotspot dots via leaflet.
  • Interactivity: tooltips, category filters via plotly.
  • Statistics (lightweight): correlation matrices (Spearman), lag checks.

Key Visualisations

  • Monthly trendlines by crime type with seasonality overlays.
  • Animated faceted charts to highlight winter/summer behaviour.
  • Interactive map of hotspots by month or category (leaflet).
  • Weather vs. crime scatter panels with smoothing to suggest associations.

Findings & Insights

  • Clear seasonal fluctuation in certain categories (e.g., anti-social behaviour higher in warmer months).
  • Weather features show weak-to-moderate associations with some crime types (interpreted cautiously; correlation ≠ causation).
  • Hotspots are spatially persistent but show month-to-month intensity shifts.
  • Interactive visuals helped stakeholders explore hypotheses quickly without heavy stats.

Limitations

  • Crime records reflect reported incidents (reporting bias possible).
  • Weather data aggregated monthly → reduces short-term signal.
  • Correlations do not imply causality; omitted variables may drive patterns.
  • Geocoding precision varies; map aggregations are approximate.

Reproducibility

  • Scripted R workflow (data load → clean → feature build → visuals).
  • Config for city bounds and date range; deterministic output given inputs.
  • Exported HTML report (RMarkdown) for sharing with non-technical users.

Next Steps

  • Add daily-level modelling and lag structures (e.g., distributed lag models).
  • Bring in mobility/events (football, school holidays) for richer context.
  • Experiment with simple predictive baselines for monthly category totals.
  • Deploy a lightweight Shiny/Quarto dashboard for stakeholders.
